# Research Design

## Method Routing

Use the claim type to choose the method:

| Claim type | Better method | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning, experience, practice, identity, interpretation | Interviews, fieldwork, case study, textual analysis | Large questionnaire without constructs |
| Attitude, perception, intention, satisfaction | Questionnaire with validated constructs | Unvalidated ad hoc items |
| Variable relation without causal identification | Cross-sectional survey, regression, SEM with theory | Causal language |
| Causal effect | Experiment, quasi-experiment, longitudinal design | Simple correlation |
| Process or mechanism in context | Case study, process tracing, grounded theory | Overgeneralized claims |
| Literature landscape | Scoping review, systematic review, bibliometric mapping | Anecdotal review |

For humanities/design/cultural research, a strong paper often uses interpretive evidence rather than large-N measurement. Do not add a questionnaire unless it answers a real research question.

## Research Questions And Hypotheses

Use RQs when:

- Concepts are exploratory or context-sensitive.
- The goal is interpretation, classification, process, or meaning.
- The data are interviews, case materials, documents, images, artifacts, or field notes.

Use hypotheses when:

- Variables are defined before data collection.
- The mechanism is grounded in theory.
- Measures, sample, and statistical tests can evaluate the expected relation.

Good RQ pattern:

`How do [actors] understand/use/negotiate [phenomenon] in [context], and what does this reveal about [theoretical or practical issue]?`

Good hypothesis pattern:

`H1: [Construct A] is positively/negatively associated with [Construct B] among [population], controlling for [controls], because [theory].`

## Theory Matrix

For manuscript-oriented work, output a compact theory matrix before instruments:

| Concept | Working definition | Candidate theory | Expected evidence | Risk if missing |
|---|---|---|---|---|

The matrix should separate empirical topic labels from analytic concepts. For craft/design/culture papers, consider concepts such as craft knowledge, heritage-as-practice, material culture, cultural sustainability, design translation, practice theory, tacit knowledge, identity, and institutional mediation. Do not claim a framework is used unless the manuscript will actually use it to interpret findings.

## Gap Claim Boundary

When using a literature landscape or journal sample to justify a gap, state the boundary:

- Database, journals, years, search strings, and screening rules.
- Whether the result proves a field-wide gap or only a target-journal gap.
- Whether coding was single-coder, double-coded, or machine-assisted.
- How ambiguous records were handled.

Avoid turning "not found in this sample" into "no one has studied this."

## Sampling

Align sampling with inference:

- **Purposive sampling** - Select information-rich participants or cases.
- **Maximum variation sampling** - Compare roles, regions, experience levels, or institutions.
- **Theoretical sampling** - Add participants/cases as emerging categories require.
- **Convenience sampling** - Acceptable only with clear limitations.
- **Representative sampling** - Do not claim unless sampling frame and selection probability support it.

Report inclusion criteria, recruitment channel, final N, refusals/dropouts if known, participant roles, and data collection dates.

For interview studies, include:

- Inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Role groups and expected evidence weight.
- Region, institution, experience level, or project-experience boundaries.
- Saturation logic or stopping rule.
- Any power relationship risks, especially when recruiting students, employees, or collaborators.

## Ethics

Always check:

- Institutional review or exemption route.
- Informed consent wording.
- Data storage and anonymization.
- Sensitive groups such as minors, students, employees, patients, or vulnerable communities.
- Whether raw data can be processed by AI tools.

Useful reporting standards:

- COREQ for interviews and focus groups.
- APA JARS for quantitative, qualitative, and mixed methods.
- STROBE for observational studies.

## Contribution Logic

A social science or humanities contribution usually takes one or more forms:

- **Theoretical** - Refines concepts, mechanisms, or boundaries.
- **Empirical** - Provides new evidence from a setting, group, archive, or field.
- **Methodological** - Demonstrates or improves a research method.
- **Practical** - Gives implications for educators, designers, policymakers, institutions, or communities.

Tie the discussion back to these contribution types explicitly.
