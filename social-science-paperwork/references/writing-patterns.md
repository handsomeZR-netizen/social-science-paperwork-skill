# Writing Patterns From Recent Social Science Papers

Use these as rhetorical patterns, not as source citations.

## High-Level Moves

1. **Title:** combine topic, setting, and mechanism. Avoid broad topic-only titles.
2. **Opening:** begin from a real social, cultural, educational, institutional, or policy tension.
3. **Gap:** specify what existing work cannot explain in this context; avoid "few studies exist" as the whole gap.
4. **Concept:** define analytic concepts before using them as explanatory tools.
5. **RQ:** use research questions for exploratory mechanisms and meaning-making; use hypotheses only for stable variable models.
6. **Method:** report sample, recruitment, data collection, ethics, analysis, and design limits.
7. **Findings:** name themes as processes or tensions, not vague topic labels.
8. **Evidence chain:** move from claim to evidence to interpretation.
9. **Discussion:** separate theoretical contribution, practical implication, and limitation.
10. **Boundary:** state transferability and context limits without over-apologizing.

## Reusable Sentence Templates

- Recent work has increasingly examined `[topic]`, yet less is known about how `[actors]` negotiate `[specific tension]` in `[setting]`.
- Existing studies have documented `[phenomenon]`, but they rarely explain how `[mechanism/process]` unfolds in `[context]`.
- This study asks how `[actors]` understand and translate `[phenomenon]` within `[context]`.
- I use `[concept]` not as a descriptive label, but as a lens for examining `[relationship/process]`.
- A qualitative design was appropriate because the study sought to understand how participants interpreted and enacted `[phenomenon]`, rather than to estimate its population prevalence.
- Participants were selected through purposive sampling to capture variation in `[role/experience/context]`.
- The first theme shows how `[actors]` treated `[phenomenon]` as `[analytic interpretation]`.
- A negative case complicates this pattern by showing that `[boundary condition]`.
- The findings extend prior work by showing that `[concept]` depends on `[condition/process]`.
- The study's transferability is limited by `[sample/context]`, but the analytic pattern may be relevant to `[adjacent settings]`.

## Structure Patterns

### Qualitative Interview Article

Problem situation -> literature gap -> conceptual lens -> RQs -> transparent methods -> thematic findings -> theory/practice discussion.

### Mixed-Methods Article

Problem needing both experience and distribution -> rationale for multiple data types -> qualitative mechanism + survey pattern -> integration -> bounded implication.

### Design/Education Article

Studio/course/practice problem -> pedagogy or design-practice concept -> intervention or case setting -> participant experience and artifacts -> learning/practice implication.

### Cultural Heritage / Craft Article

Preservation/adaptation tension -> craft as knowledge/practice/material culture -> cases/interviews/documents -> cultural and environmental sustainability distinction -> community/design/policy implication.

### Draft Demo Manuscript

Visible demo notice -> polished title and abstract -> real or plausible problem situation -> bounded literature gap -> conceptual lens -> proposed methods -> clearly labeled illustrative findings -> discussion of what the demo shows -> limits and next real-data steps.

Use this pattern only when the user explicitly wants a non-submission classroom/demo draft. Keep the writing fluent and manuscript-like, but label simulated findings and sample quotes as illustrative.

## Common Weaknesses To Avoid

- Overclaiming a target-journal gap as a field-wide gap.
- Using "impact" or "effect" without causal design.
- Treating sustainability as a slogan instead of separating environmental, cultural, and social dimensions.
- Listing themes without evidence and interpretation.
- Letting AI polish away uncertainty, conflict, and negative cases.
- Presenting Draft Demo material as if real participants, real interviews, or real statistical results existed.

## Draft Demo Phrases

- This manuscript is a classroom demonstration draft rather than a report of completed empirical research.
- The following findings are illustrative and are included to show how a qualitative findings section could be structured after real data collection.
- Simulated participant labels and sample excerpts are used only to demonstrate the evidence chain from excerpt to code to theme.
- In a real submission, this section would need to be replaced with verified participant information, anonymized transcripts, and documented coding decisions.
- The draft therefore demonstrates the logic of the study rather than claiming completed empirical evidence.
